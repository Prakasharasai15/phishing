Phishing Awareness Education

Learn how to identify and protect yourself from phishing attacks in the digital age.

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyber attack that uses disguised email addresses, websites, or messages to trick individuals into sharing sensitive information or installing malicious software. These attacks often mimic trusted entities and exploit human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities.

Did you know? The term "phishing" originated in the 1990s among hackers who were "fishing" for information from unsuspecting users.

Common Types of Phishing Attacks

Email Phishing

The most common type where attackers send fraudulent emails pretending to be from legitimate organizations.

Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks aimed at specific individuals or companies, often using personalized information.

Website Spoofing

Creating fake websites that look identical to legitimate ones to steal login credentials or financial information.

Smishing

Phishing conducted via SMS text messages, often containing malicious links.

Vishing

Voice phishing using phone calls to trick people into revealing sensitive information.

Whaling

Attacks targeting high-profile executives or high-net-worth individuals.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Suspicious Sender Email Address

    Always check the actual email address, not just the display name. Look for misspellings or suspicious domains (e.g., "support@arnazon.com" instead of "amazon.com").

  • Poor Grammar and Spelling

    Legitimate companies typically have professional communications. Multiple errors may indicate a phishing attempt.

  • Urgent or Threatening Language

    Creating a sense of urgency or fear is a common tactic to rush you into action without proper verification.

  • Suspicious Links and Attachments

    Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking. Be wary of unexpected attachments.

  • Requests for Sensitive Information

    Legitimate organizations generally don't ask for passwords, credit card numbers, or personal information via email.

How to Stay Protected

Verify Before Trusting

Always verify the source of requests for information through official channels, not through the links or contact information provided in the suspicious message.

Use PhishGuard

Scan suspicious URLs before clicking on them to determine if they're legitimate or potentially harmful.

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication

Add an extra layer of security to your accounts so that even if your credentials are compromised, attackers can't access your accounts.

Keep Software Updated

Ensure your operating system, browsers, and security software are up to date to protect against known vulnerabilities.
